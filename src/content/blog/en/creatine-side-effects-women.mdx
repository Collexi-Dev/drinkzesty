At the recommended dose of 3-10g per day, no clinically relevant side effects of creatine have been found in women. That's the conclusion from over 500 peer-reviewed studies, including the most comprehensive review ever published: the [ISSN position stand on creatine supplementation](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28615996/) (Kreider et al., 2017). Creatine is one of the most studied supplements in the world.

But that doesn't mean there aren't questions. Below, we address the most common concerns and what the research actually says.

## What is creatine, exactly?

Creatine is a substance your body naturally produces and that you also get through food (meat, fish). It's stored in your muscles and brain, where it serves as a rapid energy source for your cells.

Women naturally have [70-80% lower creatine reserves](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33800439/) than men (Smith-Ryan et al., 2021). During menopause, production drops further because estrogen plays a role in creatine synthesis ([Brosnan & Brosnan, 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17430086/)).

## The most common concerns

### Will creatine make me gain weight?

Yes, but not the way you think. Creatine draws water into your muscle cells, not under your skin. In the first 1-2 weeks, you might see 1 to 2 kg more on the scale. That's intracellular water in the muscle, not fat and not bloating.

This stabilizes after the first few weeks. Many women in menopause actually find that creatine helps them maintain muscle mass, which is better for weight management in the long run. According to [Antonio et al. (2021)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12970-021-00412-w), the weight gain from creatine is "intracellular water retention, not subcutaneous fluid."

### Will it cause stomach issues?

Stomach issues are the most commonly mentioned side effect, but they almost exclusively occur with:

- High doses on an empty stomach
- Creatine powder that doesn't dissolve properly
- The so-called "loading phase" of 20g/day (which isn't necessary)

When taken as a liquid, like a cold-pressed shot, absorption is better and stomach issues rarely occur. The [ISSN](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28615996/) concludes that gastrointestinal complaints "have not been consistently reported in controlled studies" (Kreider et al., 2017).

### Is creatine bad for my kidneys?

This is a persistent myth. Creatine raises creatinine levels in your blood. That's a breakdown product that doctors use as a marker for kidney function. But that higher creatinine doesn't come from kidney damage. It simply comes from the higher creatine intake.

Multiple long-term studies (up to 5 years) show no negative effect on kidney function in healthy adults. [Poortmans & Francaux (1999)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10449011/) demonstrated that long-term creatine supplementation does not impair kidney function in healthy athletes. The ISSN concludes: "There is no scientific evidence that creatine supplementation is harmful to the kidneys in healthy individuals."

### Does creatine affect my hormones?

No. Creatine works at the cellular energy level (the ATP system), not on the hormonal system. There is no demonstrated effect on estrogen, progesterone, or other female hormones.

There was once a single study ([van der Merwe et al., 2009](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19741313/)) that suggested an increase in DHT (dihydrotestosterone) in male rugby players. That finding has never been replicated, involved men (not women), and the measured values remained within the normal range. [Antonio et al. (2021)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12970-021-00412-w) specifically address this as a misconception.

### Does creatine cause hair loss?

This stems from that same [single DHT study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19741313/) in male athletes. There is not a single study showing a link between creatine and hair loss. Not in men, and not in women. A [recent randomized controlled trial](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/15502783.2025.2495229) (2025) found no effect on DHT, testosterone, or any hair-related outcome after 12 weeks of creatine supplementation.

## What does the major research say?

The [ISSN position stand](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28615996/) (Kreider et al., 2017) is based on hundreds of studies:

- **Safety**: Supplementation up to 30g/day for 5 years is safe and well-tolerated in healthy individuals
- **Long-term**: No side effects demonstrated with chronic use
- **Populations**: Found safe in adults of all ages, including older women

[Smith-Ryan et al. (2021)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33800439/) examined creatine specifically in women across all life stages, including menopause. They conclude that creatine has positive effects on mood, cognition, muscle mass, and bone health, without relevant side effects.

Dr. Stacy Sims, exercise physiologist and expert in women-specific supplementation, [takes creatine daily herself](https://www.drstacysims.com/newsletters/articles/posts/Why_Active_Women_Need_Creatine) and specifically recommends it for women in menopause.

## When to avoid creatine

- **With existing kidney disease**: always consult your doctor
- **With medication that affects kidney function**: consult your doctor
- **During pregnancy and breastfeeding**: insufficient research

When in doubt, talk to your doctor. Creatine monohydrate is a dietary supplement, not a medication. But your doctor knows your personal situation.
